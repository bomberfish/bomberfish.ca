If you know me personally, you'd know I'm a massive BlackBerry nerd. I have a collection of about 20 or so of their phones (a collection that actually scored me a pretty interesting opportunity I hope to cover later this year 👀)

I mean, it makes sense. Since I was 5 years old, I've lived in Waterloo, the birthplace of BlackBerry. Most of my friends had parents who worked there.

So when I saw that a small Chinese vendor was making a mod that gutted an old BlackBerry Classic and replaced the internals with what's essentially a modern low-end Android phone, I pounced on the chance. In September of last year, I ended up buying one of the full devices (not the kit). And it finally came in the mail, about ***6*** or ***7*** months later.

...sorry. Bad joke, I know. I'll see myself out.

## First Impressions

The Q25 came in practically flawless condition, however the silver midframe and rear panel were cheap non-OEM replacement parts. I was one of the later orders, so I can only assume Zinwa was starting to scrape the bottom of the barrel when it came to old BlackBerry Classics. Perfectly acceptable in my books, but I did end up getting a second Classic second-hand and swapping in those parts from it.

## Hardware

The Zinwa Technologies Q25 comes with a Mediatek [Helio G99](https://www.mediatek.com/products/smartphones/mediatek-helio-g99) (More specifically the [MT8781V/CA](https://www.mediatek.com/iot/modem-based-iot/mt8781), which is the IoT variant so expect a longer support window), 12GB of LPDDR4X (insert rampocalypse joke here), and 256GB of storage. It keeps the Classic's 720p square LCD, mono speaker, and headphone jack *(!!!)*. 

### Connectivity

Since the Q25 uses the Helio G99, it's unfortunately limited to 4G LTE. Personally, I can live with that, since as a somewhat light user of mobile data I've never really found myself wishing for faster speeds. Also, I'm still on one of my carriers' LTE plans anyway even though they aren't selling those anymore, but at least they pitied me enough to give me an extra 30GB per month.

It also only has WiFi 5 (802.11ac), but then again along with how it's LTE-only, you aren't really going to be needing more than that, since the Q25 is primarily geared towards communication. This feels like a very intentional and thought-out sacrifice for a lower price tag.

As for peripheral connectivity, it features Bluetooth 5.2 and a USB-C 2.0 port (which actually requires filing down the original microUSB hole in the chassis to fit).

### Battery Life

The original 2512mAh battery has been upgraded to a denser 3000mAh one. Generally, it can last me a whole day, with about n hours of screen-on time. 

### Cameras

The Q25 upgrades the cameras with a 50MP rear shooter, and an 8MP selfie camera.

I'll only be testing the rear camera for now. Currently, the camera is limited by some bugs in the HAL which limit it to a firmware-level binning from 50MP to 16MP and limited Camera2 support. I'll eventually make a follow-up after fixes for that are landed in LineageOS.

## Software

Out of the box, the Q25 runs Android 14, with OTAs from Zinwa bringing it up to Android 15. There aren't any plans on updating it to Android 16, which perfectly understandable for such a small vendor. 

However, in an incredibly rare and surprising move for any Android device manufacturer, Zinwa officially endorsed [a LineageOS port](https://review.lineageos.org/q/project:LineageOS/android_device_xelex_Q25) and even sent complementary devices to developers of custom ROMs and mobile Linux distributions. 

I personally didn't really like the stock ROM since it was barely-modified AOSP, so I've been running preview builds of the LineageOS 23.2 port. Once again I must stress that this is the first phone so it's not particularly reasonable to expect a really nice and polished Android skin, especially since AOSP has been sorely neglected by Google.

That being said, both the stock ROM and LineageOS ran buttery smooth, even on such a low-end SoC. Of course, this is because of the lack of all that pre-installed bloatware which plagues the low-end Android market.

Unfortunately, since Zinwa missed the certification deadline, the Q25 ships without support for Google Play Integrity, even on the stock ROM. This means certain apps require complicated workarounds to function, and forget about Google Wallet. Fortunately, I carry a wallet (crazy in the big '26, I know), and my bank's mobile app doesn't require strong integrity anyway.

Another odd quirk is that due to the unusal form factor, certain apps (such as the Play Store) see the Q25 as a tablet. This isn't usually a problem, however I did have to use Aurora Store and spoof to a different device so I could install my bank's mobile app.

## So, should you buy a Q25?

No. 

I know it's crazy that I'm saying this after glazing the hell out of this phone, but it isn't really worth it. 

It could be a nice project if you have a Classic that you want to upgrade, but if you're just itching for that physical keyboard, I'd just wait for some of the fully-original devices Zinwa is planning to release. Currently planned are the Q27 (BlackBerry Classic form factor, releasing in 2027), and the K28 (BlackBerry Key2 form factor, releasing in 2028).

Some options from other companies include the [Minimal Phone](https://minimalcompany.com/) if you want a more stripped-down experience, one of the many devices by [Unihertz](https://www.unihertz.com/collections/titan-series) (despite their... questionable ergonomics), or the [Clicks Communicator](https://clicks.tech/communicator) that might drop later this year.

Fun fact, I actually got to meet some of the people behind Communicator a few months ago! I'm pretty excited for that device, especially since they worked with the industrial designer behind the most iconic BlackBerry phones to bring forth a fresh design that's still a joy to use.
